# Action Plan – How to Actually Reduce Value-Based Churn

These aren't theoretical recommendations—they're practical changes that protect revenue by re-engaging customers before they leave.

---

## Phase 1 – Quick Wins (This Week)

These are changes you can implement immediately with minimal engineering work:

| What to Do | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| **Send automated alerts when dashboard views drop** | Users get busy and forget to check dashboards. A simple "You haven't checked your dashboard in 5 days—here's what's new" email brings them back before they completely disengage. |
| **Put "Most Used Dashboards" at the top of the home screen** | Stop making users hunt for the dashboards they check every day. Show them immediately. Reduces friction = more usage. |
| **Alert Customer Success when accounts hit HIGH RISK** | Give CS teams a daily report: "These 8 accounts are at high churn risk today." They know exactly who to call and why. |

**Why start here?** These require minimal development time but have immediate impact. You're meeting users where they are and making it easier to stay engaged.

---

## Phase 2 – Medium-Term Improvements (Next 1-2 Months)

Once quick wins are live, tackle these deeper product improvements:

| What to Do | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| **Create dashboard templates by user role** | Instead of making every new user build dashboards from scratch, give them pre-built templates. "Marketing Manager Dashboard," "Sales Director Dashboard," etc. They get value faster with less work. |
| **Add a "Data Quality Score" indicator** | One reason people stop trusting dashboards? Bad data. Show a quality score so users know if the data is reliable. Trust = usage. |
| **Build an automated "re-onboarding" flow for inactive users** | When someone hasn't logged in for 14 days, trigger a personalized email series: "Here's what you're missing," "Quick refresher on how to use dashboards," etc. Bring them back to value. |

**Why this phase matters?** You're not just nudging users back—you're removing the friction that caused them to disengage in the first place.

---

## Phase 3 – Long-Term Strategy (Next 3-6 Months)

These are bigger architectural changes that fundamentally improve retention:

| What to Build | Strategic Impact |
|---|---|
| **Predictive churn engine using multiple signals** | Right now we track dashboard usage. Next level: combine usage + feature adoption + support tickets + login patterns. Build a machine learning model that predicts churn with even higher accuracy. |
| **Auto-personalize dashboard layouts** | Instead of showing everyone the same dashboard home screen, use AI to learn what each user cares about and customize their experience. Product adapts to them = they never want to leave. |
| **Shift to value-based pricing** | Charge customers based on how much value they actually extract (dashboard views, insights generated) instead of flat rates. Aligns pricing with value = happier customers who feel they're paying fairly. |

**Why this is strategic?** You're building a retention engine that continuously learns and improves. This isn't a one-time fix—it's a sustainable competitive advantage.

## The Core Truth About Churn

Here's what most SaaS companies get wrong:

**They think churn is a billing problem or a pricing problem.**

It's not.

**Churn is a value problem.**

Customers don't leave because the product is too expensive. They leave because they're not getting enough value to justify the cost.

**The pattern is always the same:**
1. Customer stops using core features
2. Product stops delivering value
3. Customer realizes they're paying for something they don't use
4. Customer cancels

**This project proves that by measuring when customers stop using core features (like dashboards), you can:**
- Detect churn risk *before* the customer even thinks about canceling
- Intervene while there's still time to re-engage them
- Protect Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) through proactive retention
